§ 01 / PARADOX

The structure of the paradox

Since the advent of ChatGPT, AI companies have maintained an implicit understanding: we support responsible AI, we won't do "bad things." Every company has safety teams, ethics committees, and usage policies—written with great earnestness, filled with solemn pledges that their technology "will not be used for weapons of mass destruction."

But on the Iranian battlefield in March 2026, this framework faced its first true reality check, and the results were unsettling.

According to cross-verified reports from Futurism, CBS News, and CNBC, U.S. Central Command accessed Anthropic's Claude through the Palantir Maven Smart System for "evaluating intelligence, simulating wargames, and assisting in identifying strike targets." Over 1,000 targets were confirmed in the first 24 hours—one of them was an elementary school.

Anthropic's red lines: must not be used for surveillance of U.S. citizens, must not be used for fully autonomous lethal weapons.

"Assisting human operators in selecting strike targets" falls outside both of these lines.

§ 02 / COLLAPSE

Three mechanisms of
ethical framework collapse

MECHANISM 01

The loophole in definitions

Anthropic's red line is "fully autonomous lethal weapons." But a human decision chain assisted by AI—even if the AI provides 90% of the intelligence analysis and target recommendations—does not constitute "fully autonomous." This loophole is embedded in all current AI company safety policies: "keeping humans in the loop" has become a free pass for any military application.

MECHANISM 02

Path dependence of commercial logic

Anthropic → Palantir → Pentagon. Each layer can claim "we don't know the specific downstream use." Spencer Ackerman's analogy is sharp and accurate: "When you sell a lathe to Dr. Doom, what responsibility do you bear for him using it to build Doombots?"

MECHANISM 03

Political pressure and commercial incentives in resonance

Before the ban, Anthropic was effectively punished for refusing government demands. The Pentagon asked it to drop restrictions on surveillance and autonomous weapons; Anthropic refused, and was subsequently placed on the blacklist. Ethical boundaries became bargaining chips rather than genuine bottom lines.

§ 03 / DESIGNATION

A designation once
reserved for hostile nations

The Pentagon designated Anthropic as a "Supply Chain Risk." This designation had previously been used only for China's Huawei and Russia's Kaspersky—companies seen as foreign adversarial entities.

Designating a U.S.-based AI company as a supply chain risk carries symbolic significance far beyond the practical ban itself:

  • Sending a clear signal to the entire defense contractor ecosystem: companies using Claude would need to prove to the DOD that their supply chain was "free of Anthropic contamination"
  • Forcing defense clients like Palantir to take a public stance—CEO Alex Karp still insisted on using Claude at the time
  • More small and mid-sized defense tech companies chose to immediately sever connections with Anthropic—the risk of losing DOD certification was unbearable

This is the first time an AI company experienced firsthand: standing by ethical principles carries a commercial cost, and that cost comes from the clients most capable of paying.

§ 04 / IMPLICATIONS

Three layers of implications

FOR · AI COMPANIES

"Usage policies" do not equal "real constraints." Anthropic's case shows that red lines must be enforced at the contractual level (not just the ToS level) and at the technical level (capability restrictions / monitoring) simultaneously—otherwise they are merely PR documents.

FOR · ENTERPRISES PROCURING AI

A supplier's political risk is becoming a new variable in procurement decisions. If your business involves government contracts, your AI vendor's relationship with the government may directly affect your qualifications. This is a risk dimension that almost no enterprise considered before 2025.

FOR · AI GOVERNANCE

This case is likely to become the flashpoint for international discussions on AI arms control. The EU AI Act's military exemption clause and UN-level initiatives on rules for AI in warfare will both be revisited using the Claude-Iran incident as a reference frame.

§ 05 / FIRST FRACTURE

Not the end—
the beginning

Anthropic is currently seeking a temporary injunction in court to suspend the Pentagon's supply chain risk designation. The outcome of this legal battle will directly determine: whether commercial AI companies have the right to refuse government clients on ethical grounds without suffering government retaliation.

That same week, AIUC (AI insurance company) released the AIUC-1 standard, and ElevenLabs became the first company to obtain AI agent insurance. This is no coincidence—the emergence of insurance and certification standards is itself a systemic signal that "AI is bearing real-world consequences."

The problem of AI weaponization will not disappear regardless of the outcome of this lawsuit.
This is merely the first crack.
