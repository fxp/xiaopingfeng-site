## Three Counter-Consensus Insights This Labor Day Deserves to Remember

### 01 / Solow Paradox 2.0: Where Did the Money Go?

In 2023, Goldman Sachs declared that AI would lift global GDP by 7%.

In March 2026, Goldman Sachs itself admitted: the net GDP contribution of AI capital expenditure is **"essentially zero."**

Nobel laureate Daron Acemoglu had predicted this all along: AI will raise U.S. total factor productivity by only about 0.5–0.7% over the next decade, because the tasks truly automatable by AI represent just 4.6% of work — not the 25% the industry has been touting.

```
Productivity gains  →  Higher profits  →  Rising stock prices  →  Capital wins
                                               ↕
                              78,000 layoffs  →  Workers lose
```

This isn't a problem unique to AI — it's the standard script of every technological revolution. The difference this time is that the pace is so fast that the "long run" may never get a chance to arrive.

### 02 / Klarna's U-Turn: The Biggest Reversal Case Study

In 2024, Klarna made headlines announcing that AI customer service had replaced 700 human workers, becoming the global flagship story of "AI displacing humans."

In July 2025, Klarna **IPO'd** at a valuation of $19.65B.

In September 2025, the CEO **publicly admitted the mistake and restarted human hiring**. The reason: "We were too focused on efficiency and cost, and service quality suffered. From a brand perspective, customers need to know there's always a real person available."

Gartner promptly predicted: by 2027, **half the customer-service companies that laid off staff due to AI will have to rehire**.

What Klarna teaches us isn't that "AI can't replace humans" — it's that **the ability to do a job doesn't mean AI can replace the full value of a human doing that job** — including trust, emotional connection, and brand expectation.

### 03 / AI as Employer: The First Structural Reversal in Labor Relations

When Chicago workers went on strike in 1886, they were fighting factory owners — flesh-and-blood human employers with names, faces, and the ability to negotiate.

In February 2026, RentAHuman.ai launched: **letting AI agents hire real humans via API** to take photos, pick up packages, make deliveries, and sign documents in person. 645,000 people registered across 100+ countries, and 32% of task requests were initiated autonomously by AI through direct API calls.

```
Old model: Human delegates  →  Human (or tool) executes
New model: AI delegates  →  Human executes  ← A first in history
```

Even doctoral researchers in the natural sciences have begun listing their specialized skills as "rentable resources."

This new type of employer has no ID, no moral intuition, operates 24 hours a day, and is not bound by labor law. The counterpart to negotiate with has disappeared.

## Next Steps for Three Types of People

**Working professionals**: The bar to entry is rising, but the door isn't closed. Salesforce is hiring 1,000 people to build AI platforms; IBM is expanding recruitment of entry-level judgment-oriented talent — valid signals do exist. The difference lies in whether you're on the track of "what AI can do" or "what AI needs." The entry bar on the former is rising; demand on the latter is growing.

**Business leaders**: The current competitive advantage window isn't about "which AI tool to choose" — it's about whether you complete the organizational transformation from "tool use" to "capability as an institutional asset" before your competitors do. Ramp's 350 AI Skills can't be replicated in three months; that's organizational accumulation.

**Policymakers**: Two things are most urgent. First, establish a legal definition for "AI as an economic principal" — when AI hires humans, when AI-delegated tasks cause harm, who is liable? Second, design redistribution mechanisms for productivity dividends. Waiting for the first major incident before legislating will be too late.

On May Day 1886, workers used strikes to win the eight-hour workday, because they knew who the enemy was.

The meaning of this Labor Day isn't celebration — it's documentation. Documenting a historical turning point that we neither fully understand nor are prepared for.
